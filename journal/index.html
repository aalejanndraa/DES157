Journal #3

February 10, 2026

While looking through “10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading & Visual Thinking Skills” from The New York Times Learning Network, I was reminded of what I call visual thinking — slowing down.

The three open-ended questions of “What’s going on?”, “What do you see that makes you say that?”, and “What more can you find?” were easy to understand, however they require evidence-based observations. As a designer, I recognize this is exactly how I critique my own work — not only responding emotionally, but supporting my responses with visual evidence. This reinforces my belief that images require active reading; they provide meaning beyond what they appear to show.

For inspiration, I explored Mr. Panda’s Psychologically Safe Portfolio. The website uses soft illustrations, playful animation, and interactive storytelling to communicate personality and values. Its strongest quality is how visual and interactive elements reinforce the concept of psychological safety through rounded shapes, warm colors, and gentle movement. One drawback is that some animations slightly detract from readability, which could overwhelm users. Overall, image and interaction are most effective when conceptually aligned rather than purely decorative.

Journal #2

January 29, 2026

In Naema Baskanderi’s article, it points out how modal windows can be beneficial but are frequently overused. Modals are designed to call attention and help users stay on task; however, users have been conditioned to rapidly dismiss them since they interrupt the workflow. Another important point I took away from the article was the need for users to have a clear mechanism to “escape” from modal dialogs, such as a visible close button or the escape key. The author also provided valuable insights into accessibility, including keyboard controls for managing focus within modals. Overall, the article made me think about how modals should be initiated by users and used purposefully rather than automatically.

Journal #1

January 20, 2026

After reading Salim Ansari’s “Best Practices for Form Design,” I began to see how the thoughtful structure of a form affects completion rates. By reducing the information required to only what is necessary, you reduce cognitive load and make the form feel quicker to complete. When users feel less overwhelmed, they are more likely to finish the form.

I also found it interesting that one of the best practices for forms is using a single-column layout with labels above the input field. This creates a smoother flow and minimizes confusion. Clear error messages also stood out to me because they help users correct mistakes without feeling blamed.

A good example of a form that follows these best practices is the Apple account sign-up form. It is clearly laid out, uses everyday language, and provides straightforward directions.

Article link: Best practices for form design

Form example: Apple ID sign-up
