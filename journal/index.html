Journal #1

February 11, 2026

After reading Salim Ansari’s “Best Practices for Form Design,” I began to see how the overall thoughtful structure of a form affects how completed it gets (specifically the number of input fields). By reducing the amount of information needed to only what is required, you reduce the cognitive load on the user and give them the feeling of finishing the form quickly. When there are fewer times that a user feels overwhelmed (from having an excessive number of inputs), the user will also have a better experience with the form. I have been frustrated in the past by forms that required excessive amounts of unnecessary information.

I also found it interesting that one of the best practices for forms is to use a single column layout with the labels above the input field. This allows for a smooth flow through the form and minimizes confusion. Additionally, the section discussing error messages stood out to me. Providing concise and clear feedback helps users correct errors without placing blame on them.

A good example of a form that follows these best practices is the Apple account sign-up form. It is clearly laid out, uses everyday language, and provides straightforward directions. This article helped me realize that good form design emphasizes clarity, efficiency, and user trust rather than aesthetics alone.
